# Is It AI? — Fair Use and Copyright Compliance Policy

## About This Policy

This policy covers how "Is It AI?" handles copyrighted material. The app is a non-commercial university capstone project that teaches users to tell AI-generated images apart from real photographs. It will never carry ads, in-app purchases, or any form of monetization.

---

## Fair Use Statement

This application may contain copyrighted material that has not been specifically authorized by the copyright holders. The material is used to teach media literacy — specifically, the skill of identifying AI-generated images. This is a different purpose from why the photographs were originally created (journalism, art, commercial use, etc.).

Each copyrighted image appears alongside educational commentary: artifact annotations, explanations of what makes the image look real or artificial, photographer attribution, and the story behind the image. The app does not compete with any market for the original photographs.

This use is consistent with Title 17, Section 107 of the United States Code (fair use), and with the Code of Best Practices in Fair Use for Media Literacy Education published by the Center for Media and Social Impact at American University.

We do not support any use of materials from this application beyond fair use.

---

## How the Four Fair Use Factors Apply

**Purpose and character.** The app is non-commercial and educational. It uses photographs as subjects of analysis, not as decorative content. Courts have held that educational uses serving a different purpose than the original work weigh in favor of fair use. See *Keck v. Mix Creative Learning Center*, No. 23-20188 (5th Cir. 2024), which found educational use of copyrighted artwork was fair use even in a commercial context.

**Nature of the works.** Photographs are creative works, which usually weighs against fair use. However, the app uses them not for their artistic value but to analyze their visual properties.

**Amount used.** The app displays full images because the educational goal requires it. A user cannot learn to identify AI artifacts in a cropped or partial image. Courts have accepted this reasoning. See *Nunez v. Caribbean Int'l News Corp.*, 235 F.3d 18 (1st Cir. 2000): "copying any less would make the picture useless to the story."

**Market effect.** The app does not substitute for any existing market for these photographs. No licensing market exists for "AI detection quiz" use of photographs.

## Where Images Come From

The app prefers freely licensed content over fair use claims. In order of preference:

For real photographs:
1. CC0 or public domain images (Pexels, Wikimedia Commons)
2. Creative Commons licensed images with proper attribution
3. Copyrighted photographs under fair use, only when a specific image is needed as an educational case study and no licensed alternative exists

For AI-generated images:
- Images generated by the development team using paid accounts (Midjourney, DALL-E, Adobe Firefly)
- Public domain AI images (AI-generated works are not eligible for copyright protection under current U.S. law, per the U.S. Copyright Office)
- CC0 datasets like DiffusionDB

Each copyrighted image used under fair use is selected because it is directly relevant to teaching AI detection, cannot be replaced with a licensed alternative, and is accompanied by educational commentary.

## Attribution

All images are credited to their creators. Attribution appears in the app's feedback screens and credits section, and includes the creator's name, year, source, and license type where applicable. Creative Commons works follow the TASL format (Title, Author, Source, License).

## Content Removal (DMCA)

If a copyright holder believes their work has been used beyond fair use, they can request removal.

Designated DMCA Agent:
Kevin Rutledge
California State Polytechnic University, San Luis Obispo
Email: krutledg@calpoly.edu

A valid takedown notice must include:
- Identification of the copyrighted work claimed to be infringed
- Identification of the material to be removed (e.g., which image in the app)
- Contact information for the complainant
- A statement of good faith belief that the use is not authorized
- A statement under penalty of perjury that the information is accurate
- A physical or electronic signature

On receiving a valid notice, I will:
1. Acknowledge it within 72 hours
2. Review the claim
3. Remove the content promptly if the claim has merit, or explain the fair use basis if I believe the use is lawful
4. Notify the complainant of the outcome

If any rights holder asks for their work to be removed, I will honor that request regardless of fair use analysis.

I reserve the right to file a counter-notification under 17 U.S.C. § 512(g) if content is removed based on mistake or misidentification.

Repeat infringement: If a content contributor repeatedly provides material that results in valid takedown notices, their contributions will be removed from the app.

DMCA agent registration: The designated agent is registered with the U.S. Copyright Office DMCA Designated Agent Directory at https://dmca.copyright.gov, as required for safe harbor protection under 17 U.S.C. § 512.

## Guidelines for Adding Content

Anyone adding images to the app should:

1. Record the source of every image (URL, creator, license)
2. Write a fair use justification for any copyrighted image without a license
3. Use licensed content when a comparable image is available
4. Write educational commentary for every image
5. Use lower resolution than the original where possible
6. Do not use images from sources that prohibit educational reuse
